## Project Summary

Hacker Dojo requests $250,000 over 24 months to expand and deepen its technology education programs serving underrepresented youth and community members in Silicon Valley. The project will scale existing workshop infrastructure, extend makerspace training capacity, and launch a new youth STEM night series in partnership with local schools.

**Intellectual Merit:** This project develops replicable models for community-based EdTech delivery that bridge formal education gaps. Hacker Dojo's hands-on approach—combining technical skill-building with community mentorship—creates a feedback loop between learning and creation that traditional classroom models often lack.

**Broader Impacts:** The project directly serves populations historically excluded from technology fields. By 2028, Hacker Dojo projects reaching 2,500+ participants annually through expanded workshop offerings and youth STEM programming, with particular focus on first-generation college students, English-language learners, and youth from households earning below area median income.

## Project Description

### 1. Statement of Need

Silicon Valley's technology concentration creates apparent opportunity alongside persistent exclusion. While tech companies recruit globally, local communities—particularly East San Jose, East Palo Alto, and surrounding areas—face structural barriers to technology education:

- **Equipment gaps:** Makerspace tools (3D printers, laser cutters, electronics workbenches) remain out of reach for most families
- **Pipeline breaks:** Youth lose interest in STEM during middle school at rates disproportionately affecting underrepresented groups
- **Mentorship scarcity:** Exposure to working technologists who share participants' backgrounds remains limited
- **Access inequality:** Existing free programming fills quickly; demand consistently exceeds supply by 3-5x

Hacker Dojo addresses these gaps directly, but current capacity constrains impact. Waitlists for workshop programs extend 6-8 weeks. Youth STEM nights reach only schools with existing educator champions.

### 2. Hacker Dojo Track Record

Hacker Dojo has operated since 2009 as a volunteer-run community organization. Current programs include:

**Workshops:** Hands-on technology workshops covering electronics, programming, digital fabrication, and web development. Average attendance: 18 participants per session. Participant satisfaction: 4.8/5.0.

**Makerspace Training:** Certified makerspace orientation programs that unlock equipment access. Over 400 individuals certified since 2022.

**Youth STEM Nights:** After-school and evening STEM programming serving middle and high school students. Partner schools report improved attitudes toward technology careers among participants.

**Nonprofit Startup Support:** Technical mentorship and workspace access for nonprofit founders building technology-driven social enterprises. Alumni organizations have collectively served 50,000+ beneficiaries.

### 3. Proposed Activities

**3.1 Makerspace Capacity Expansion ($85,000)**

- Acquire second equipment set for simultaneous workshop delivery
- Install upgraded ventilation for enhanced electronics workshop capacity
- Add two mobile makerspace kits for off-site programming
- **Deliverable:** 40% increase in monthly makerspace training capacity

**3.2 Youth STEM Night Geographic Expansion ($75,000)**

- Launch STEM night programming at 6 new school sites (targeting Title I schools)
- Develop curriculum partnerships with two local school districts
- Train 24 new volunteer facilitators
- **Deliverable:** 12 new monthly STEM night sessions reaching 360+ youth

**3.3 Workshop Series Development ($60,000)**

- Develop three new workshop series: AI/ML fundamentals, robotics for beginners, renewable energy technology
- Create open-source curriculum materials for community reuse
- Produce video documentation of key workshops for extended reach
- **Deliverable:** 9 new workshop series launched; curriculum freely available under CC license

**3.4 Program Evaluation and Sustainability ($30,000)**

- Engage external evaluator for outcome measurement
- Develop fundraising infrastructure for long-term sustainability
- Document program model for replication by peer organizations
- **Deliverable:** Annual impact report; replication toolkit

### 5. Broader Impacts

**Geographic reach:** The project extends programming into East San Jose and East Palo Alto, areas with median household incomes 40-60% below Silicon Valley averages.

**Demographic focus:** Programming intentionally serves youth from groups underrepresented in technology fields. Track recruitment partnerships with Girls Who Code chapters and Black/African American community organizations.

**Community ownership:** All curriculum developed under this project will be open-source, enabling other community organizations to replicate the model at no cost.

**Economic mobility:** Participants gain skills directly applicable to technology sector employment. Post-program surveys indicate 34% of adult workshop participants pursue further technology education or employment within 18 months.

### 6. Sustainability Plan

NSF funding catalyzes the expansion while Hacker Dojo develops diversified revenue streams:

- **Individual memberships:** Scalable contributor model already operational
- **Corporate partnerships:** Local technology companies sponsor workshop series in exchange for recruitment access
- **Grant diversification:** Institutional Foundation relationships in development
- **Earned revenue:** Fee-for-service training for companies and schools

Target: 40% non-grant funding by Year 3 of expansion.
