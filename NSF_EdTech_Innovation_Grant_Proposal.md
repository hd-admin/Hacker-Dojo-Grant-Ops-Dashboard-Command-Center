## Project Summary

Hacker Dojo requests $250,000 over 24 months to expand technology education and community innovation programs serving underrepresented youth in Silicon Valley. The project will scale existing workshop infrastructure, launch a new Youth STEM Pathway Program, and develop replicable curriculum for community makerspaces nationwide.

**Intellectual Merit:** This project develops an evidence-based model for community-based EdTech education that bridges formal schooling gaps through hands-on makerspace learning, addressing NSF's priority area of broadening participation in STEM.

**Broader Impacts:** The project will directly serve 1,200+ youth from underserved communities, train 40+ community volunteers as STEM educators, and produce open-source curriculum replicated by 10+ partner organizations by project end.

## 1. Statement of Need

### 1.1 The Problem

Silicon Valley's technology economy has created extraordinary opportunity—and extraordinary exclusion. While tech companies recruit globally, local communities in East San Jose, Redwood City, and surrounding areas see limited pathways into technology careers. Youth from low-income families, first-generation college students, and underrepresented minorities face three interconnected barriers:

**Access gaps.** School districts serving predominantly minority populations allocate 40% less per student for STEM programming than wealthier districts in the same metropolitan area. Many schools lack basic makerspace equipment, let alone integrated EdTech curriculum.

**Relevance gaps.** Standard computer science education often fails to connect to students' communities and lived experiences. Youth see technology as something consumed, not created—something that happens in distant corporate campuses rather than their own neighborhoods.

**Network gaps.** Even talented students lack connections to mentors, internship opportunities, and professional networks that translate technical skills into careers. The informal economy of tech mentorship favors those already embedded in professional networks.

### 1.2 The Opportunity

Community makerspaces occupy a unique position to address these gaps. Unlike schools constrained by curriculum standards and testing pressures, community spaces can experiment with project-based learning that connects technology to real-world problems. Unlike corporate diversity programs that often serve a narrow slice of already-advantaged students, community organizations reach youth where they already gather.

Hacker Dojo has operated at this intersection since its founding, with documented success in translating community presence into technology pathways. But demand now exceeds capacity: current programs serve 200 youth monthly against a waitlist of 600+, and organizational capacity limits the organization's ability to respond.

### 1.3 Organizational Track Record

Hacker Dojo's 2022–2025 programming produced measurable outcomes:
- 73% of youth participants reported increased interest in technology careers
- 89% of workshop graduates demonstrated measurable skill gains (pre/post assessment)
- 24 alumni entered post-secondary STEM programs or technology apprenticeships
- 40 community volunteers contributed 3,200+ hours of teaching and mentorship
- Partnerships with 8 regional school districts established referral pathways

## 2. Project Objectives

This project pursues four interconnected objectives:

**Objective 1: Scale existing workshop capacity by 150%.** Expand monthly workshop throughput from 200 to 500 youth participants through additional programming hours, instructor training, and equipment procurement.

**Objective 2: Launch the Youth STEM Pathway Program.** Develop a 9-month cohort-based program for 15–18 year olds combining advanced makerspace training, industry mentorship, and college/career navigation. Target enrollment: 60 students annually.

**Objective 3: Build replicable curriculum.** Document and package existing Hacker Dojo curriculum as open-source modules suitable for adoption by community makerspaces nationwide. Target: 15 curriculum modules covering electronics, programming, and digital fabrication.

**Objective 4: Establish a regional training institute.** Train 40+ community volunteers and educators from partner organizations in evidence-based EdTech instruction methods, creating a sustainable pipeline of qualified instructors.

## 3. Project Design

### 3.1 Program Architecture

The project comprises three integrated program tracks operating concurrently:

**Track A: Expanded Open Workshop Program**

Hacker Dojo's signature drop-in workshop model provides hands-on exposure to technology tools in a low-pressure, high-creativity environment. The grant will fund:
- Extended operating hours (adding 20 hours/week of open lab time)
- New equipment: 3D printers (5), laser cutters (2), electronics workbenches (10), VR development stations (4)
- Stipended instructor hours to supplement volunteer capacity
- Updated curriculum covering: 3D modeling and printing, microcontroller programming (Arduino/Raspberry Pi), web development, and digital citizenship

**Track B: Youth STEM Pathway Program**

A structured 9-month cohort experience for older youth:
- *Months 1–3:* Foundations—advanced electronics, programming fundamentals, design thinking methodology
- *Months 4–6:* Specialization—students select focus tracks (fabrication, software, robotics)
- *Months 7–9:* Applied project—teams solve real problems provided by community partners, culminating in public demonstration
- Each student paired with a professional mentor from a tech company or startup
- Participants receive monthly stipends ($200) to remove economic barriers to sustained engagement

**Track C: Regional Training Institute**

A train-the-trainer program building regional capacity:
- 80-hour instructor certification program (8 weeks, part-time)
- Curriculum delivery using the open-source modules developed under Track A
- Ongoing coaching and professional development for program graduates
- Target: 40 certified instructors from 15+ community organizations

### 3.2 Theory of Change

*If* community youth gain hands-on experience with technology tools *and* develop relationships with working professionals *and* receive structured support for post-secondary pathways, *then* they will be more likely to pursue technology careers *because* they will have both the skills and the networks to succeed.

This theory is grounded in research on STEM identity development, which demonstrates that career aspirations correlate more strongly with perceived belonging and access to role models than with academic performance alone.

### 3.3 Participant Engagement

Hacker Dojo uses a multi-channel recruitment strategy to reach underrepresented youth:
- **School partnerships:** Direct referral from STEM teachers and counselors at partner districts
- **Community outreach:** Partnerships with libraries, youth shelters, and community centers in target zip codes
- **Peer networks:** Alumni ambassador program leverages existing participants to reach their peers
- **Digital channels:** Targeted social media campaigns in English, Spanish, and Vietnamese

### 3.4 Community Partnership Model

- **Advisory Board:** Includes youth representatives, parent guardians, school district partners, and local employers
- **Community Problem Bank:** Real-world challenges submitted by local organizations shape student project topics
- **Final Showcase:** Public demonstration event where students present projects to community members, family, and potential employers

## 4. Evaluation Plan

### 4.1 Evaluation Framework

The project uses a mixed-methods evaluation approach measuring both program implementation and participant outcomes.

**Process Evaluation (ongoing):**
- Monthly attendance tracking and participant demographics
- Instructor feedback surveys after each session

**Outcome Evaluation (baseline and annual):**
- Pre/post assessments of technical skills (validated instruments)
- STEM identity surveys (established instruments adapted from existing literature)
