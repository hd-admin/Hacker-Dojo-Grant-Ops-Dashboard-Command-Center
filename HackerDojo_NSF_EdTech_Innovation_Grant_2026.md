## Project Summary

Hacker Dojo requests $250,000 over 24 months to expand technology education access across underserved communities in Silicon Valley through an integrated program of hands-on workshops, makerspace training, youth STEM engagement events, and nonprofit technology startup support.

The project addresses a critical gap: while Silicon Valley generates global technology innovation, surrounding communities—particularly first-generation learners, low-income youth, and underrepresented populations—lack access to the spaces, tools, and mentorship needed to participate in the technology economy. Hacker Dojo bridges this gap by providing free and low-cost programming that transforms passive technology consumers into active creators and innovators.

**Proposed Activities:**
- Weekly hands-on technology workshops (3D printing, electronics, coding, web development)
- Structured makerspace training with certification pathways
- Monthly Youth STEM Nights engaging 200+ middle and high school students
- Accelerator programming for nonprofit technology startups serving educational missions
- Mentorship matching between industry professionals and program participants

**Expected Outcomes:**
- 1,500+ unduplicated program participants
- 75% of youth participants demonstrating increased STEM interest
- 30 nonprofit organizations receiving technical support
- 500+ hours of volunteer mentor engagement
- Establishment of two new community makerspace partnerships

**Intellectual Merit:** This project contributes a replicable model for community-based technology education that integrates formal learning objectives with informal maker culture, providing empirical evidence on effective EdTech delivery to underserved populations.

**Broader Impacts:** By lowering barriers to technology education, this project advances NSF's mission to promote science and technology education while supporting economic mobility in communities historically excluded from the innovation economy.

## Project Description

### 1. Statement of Need

Silicon Valley's technology industry employs over 400,000 workers and generates billions in economic activity annually. Yet access to technology education remains sharply unequal. Youth from households earning under $50,000 annually are 80% less likely to own a computer adequate for coding education and 60% less likely to have access to internet connectivity sufficient for online learning.

Hacker Dojo has observed these disparities directly through its existing programming. Since 2018, the organization has served over 4,000 participants through workshops and events, with demand consistently outstripping capacity. Waitlists for core programs extend 8-12 weeks. Exit surveys indicate that 70% of youth participants report no prior access to similar programming at their schools.

The consequences extend beyond individual opportunity. Nonprofit organizations serving these communities frequently lack technical capacity to build and maintain the digital infrastructure their clients depend on. Community centers, workforce development organizations, and youth-serving nonprofits often operate on shoestring budgets without dedicated technology staff, limiting their ability to leverage digital tools effectively.

**The core problem:** Existing technology education infrastructure in Silicon Valley serves those already positioned to succeed, while underrepresented youth and mission-driven nonprofits lack access to the physical spaces, equipment, and mentorship required to build technology fluency.

### 2. Project Objectives

This project pursues four primary objectives aligned with NSF's commitment to broadening participation in STEM:

**Objective 1: Expand Hands-On Workshop Capacity**
Deliver 200+ technology workshops over 24 months, increasing total participant capacity by 40% over current operations. Workshops will cover introductory and intermediate topics in electronics, programming, digital fabrication, and web development, with curriculum designed for adult learners and youth ages 12-18.

**Objective 2: Establish Structured Makerspace Certification**
Develop and launch a tiered makerspace certification program that provides formal skills validation for equipment usage (3D printers, laser cutters, CNC routers, electronics workbenches). Certification creates progression pathways that motivate continued engagement and signal competency to employers.

**Objective 3: Scale Youth STEM Night Programming**
Increase Youth STEM Night frequency from quarterly to monthly events, with targeted outreach to Title I middle schools in San Jose, East Palo Alto, and Redwood City. Each event will engage 100-150 students through rotating interactive demonstrations led by volunteer engineers and makers.

**Objective 4: Build Nonprofit Technology Capacity**
Launch a dedicated nonprofit technology support track providing pro bono technical consulting, website development, database management, and digital security services to organizations serving underrepresented communities. Goal: 30 organizations served over the grant period.

### 3. Project Work Plan

**Phase 1: Foundation Building (Months 1-6)**
- Hire and onboard Program Coordinator (1.0 FTE)
- Procure additional equipment: 4 desktop 3D printers, 2 electronics workbenches, computing stations for 20 concurrent users
- Develop curriculum for 6 workshop tracks aligned with community needs assessment
- Establish partnerships with 5 community organizations for referral and outreach
- Launch website redesign supporting online registration and program tracking

**Phase 2: Program Delivery (Months 7-18)**
- Deliver 120+ workshops across all tracks
- Host 10 Youth STEM Night events with average attendance of 125 students
- Initiate makerspace certification pilot with 50 participants
- Begin nonprofit technology consulting engagements (first 15 organizations)
- Launch mentorship matching platform connecting 100+ volunteer mentors with participants

**Phase 3: Expansion and Evaluation (Months 19-24)**
- Deliver remaining 80+ workshops
- Complete makerspace certification for 150+ participants
- Finalize nonprofit consulting engagements (15 additional organizations)
- Conduct comprehensive program evaluation with external evaluator
- Document curriculum, operational playbooks, and partnership frameworks for replication
- Publish evaluation report and host community showcase event

### 4. Evaluation Plan

Hacker Dojo will engage an independent external evaluator to assess program effectiveness across three dimensions:

**Participation Metrics:**
- Total unduplicated participants served
- Demographic breakdown (age, gender, ethnicity, income level, zip code)
- Program completion rates
- Return participation rates (participants attending 3+ sessions)

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Pre/post assessments for skill-based workshops
- Youth participant STEM attitude surveys (validated instrument)
- Certification passage rates for makerspace program
- Nonprofit partner satisfaction surveys

**Community Impact:**
- Volunteer hours contributed
- Employer/higher education placement of program alumni (12-month follow-up)
- Community organization capacity metrics (websites launched, systems improved)
- Replication inquiries from other organizations

The evaluator will provide semi-annual reports enabling adaptive management throughout the project period.

### 5. Organizational Qualifications

Hacker Dojo brings established infrastructure, community trust, and demonstrated capacity to execute this project:

**Track Record:** Since 2018, Hacker Dojo has operated a 4,000 sq. ft. makerspace and community center in Mountain View, serving over 4,000 participants through workshops, events, and mentorship programming. The organization maintains a 4.7/5.0 average rating across 200+ Google Reviews, with particular praise for accessible pricing and welcoming environment.

**Staff:** The proposed Program Coordinator will bring 5+ years of experience in community education and program management. Workshop instruction is supplemented by a volunteer corps of 50+ industry professionals who contribute teaching time on a rotating basis.

**Community Partnerships:** Hacker Dojo maintains active partnerships with the Mountain View Public Library, San Jose State University College of Engineering, and five Title I school districts. These relationships provide participant referral pipelines and ensure programming aligns with community needs.

**Financial Management:** Hacker Dojo maintains clean annual audits, currently holds $180,000 in operational reserves (approximately 6 months of expenses), and has never received a disallowed cost determination from any funder.

### 6. Sustainability Plan

NSF funding will enable Hacker Dojo to build infrastructure and demonstrate program model viability. Sustainability beyond the grant period relies on three revenue streams:

**Earned Revenue:** Workshop fees ($15-50 per session) for participants who can pay, generating approximately $45,000 annually at projected volumes. Certification fees offset equipment maintenance costs.

**Foundation and Corporate Partnerships:** A dedicated grants writer will pursue funding from regional foundations and corporate giving programs (Google.org, Silicon Valley Community Foundation, Panasci Family Foundation) to support ongoing operations.

**Membership Model:** Post-grant, a tiered membership model will provide unlimited workshop access for $50/month, with scholarship tiers ensuring no one is excluded for inability to pay. Projected membership revenue: $60,000 annually at 100 active members.

The organization commits to maintaining free and reduced-cost access for 60%+ of participants regardless of funding source.
