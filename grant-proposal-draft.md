### Vision Statement

Hacker Dojo proposes to expand access to technology education and community innovation in Silicon Valley through an integrated suite of maker education programs targeting underserved youth, adult learners transitioning to tech careers, and nonprofit organizations building social impact.

### Intellectual Merit

This project delivers a replicable model for community-based STEM education that bridges formal schooling gaps. Our approach combines hands-on makerspace training with structured curriculum alignment, enabling participants to develop practical skills in robotics, electronics, and digital fabrication while building STEM identity.

### Broader Impacts

- Serve 500+ youth annually through weekly STEM workshops and monthly maker nights
- Train 50 adult learners per year in technical skills for workforce transition
- Support 25 nonprofit startups per year with technical mentorship and resource access
- Develop open-source curriculum replicable across 100+ maker spaces nationwide

## 1. PROJECT DESCRIPTION

### 1.1 Statement of Need

Silicon Valley's technology boom has widened educational disparities. Families with resources access enrichment; those without fall behind. Hacker Dojo addresses this inequity directly—providing free and low-cost access to tools, training, and community that most would otherwise lack.

**The problem:**

- Traditional STEM education lacks hands-on technical components
- Makerspace access remains geographically and financially restricted
- Nonprofits building social impact lack technical support structures
- Youth from underrepresented backgrounds face barriers to tech career pathways

**Evidence of demand:**

Hacker Dojo currently runs weekly youth STEM nights, weekend workshops, and ongoing makerspace training. Programs consistently sell out, with waitlists spanning months—a direct signal of unmet demand.

### 1.2 Project Objectives

**Primary Objectives (24-month grant period):**

| Objective | Metric | Target |
|-----------|--------|--------|
| Youth STEM Participation | Unique participants | 500/year |
| Adult Workforce Training | Credentials earned | 100 |
| Nonprofit Capacity Building | Organizations served | 25/year |
| Curriculum Development | Open-source modules | 20 modules |
| Geographic Reach | Partner locations | 5 sites |

**Secondary Objectives:**

- Establish regional maker education network connecting 10+ Bay Area organizations
- Pilot a train-the-trainer program for volunteer instructor development
- Create assessment framework measuring STEM identity development

### 1.3 Program Design

**Program 1: Youth STEM Nights (Ages 8-18)**

Weekly sessions combining structured instruction with open lab time. Participants progress through tiered skill badges in electronics, programming, and digital fabrication. Culminates in a community showcase twice yearly.

**Program 2: Adult Workforce Transition**

12-week intensive tracks in collaboration with local employers. Curriculum aligned to industry-recognized credentials. Includes internship placement with partner organizations.

**Program 3: Nonprofit Innovation Support**

Pro bono technical mentorship, access to makerspace tools, and startup advisory services. Priority given to organizations addressing climate, health, and education access.

**Program 4: Open-Source Curriculum**

All curriculum developed will be documented and released under Creative Commons license. Includes lesson plans, slide decks, project guides, and assessment rubrics.

### 1.4 Theory of Change

```
INPUTS: Funding, volunteer instructors, facility space, equipment
    ↓
ACTIVITIES: Workshops, training programs, mentorship, curriculum development
    ↓
OUTPUTS: Participants trained, credentials earned, organizations supported
    ↓
SHORT-TERM OUTCOMES: Skills acquisition, STEM identity development, career pathways initiated
    ↓
LONG-TERM OUTCOMES: Increased diversity in tech workforce, stronger nonprofit sector, scalable model replicated
```

### 1.5 Evaluation Plan

**Formative Evaluation (Ongoing):**

- Pre/post surveys measuring STEM attitudes and self-efficacy
- Monthly attendance tracking and engagement metrics
- Instructor observation protocols
- Participant feedback loops

**Summative Evaluation (Annual):**

- Third-party assessment of skill gains using validated instruments
- Longitudinal tracking of participant pathways (12-month follow-up)
- Cost-per-participant analysis
- Model replicability assessment

### 1.6 Dissemination

- Quarterly public reports published on Hacker Dojo website
- Annual conference presentations at MAKER Faires and STEM education convenings
- Open-source curriculum repository with documentation
- Partnership with academic researchers studying maker education

## 2. BROADER IMPACTS

### 2.1 Impact on STEM Workforce Development

The technology sector faces persistent talent pipeline challenges, particularly in diversifying the workforce. Hacker Dojo addresses this by creating early and sustained exposure to technical fields for youth from backgrounds historically excluded from tech.

Research shows that sustained maker participation correlates with increased STEM identity and career interest, particularly for girls and students of color. Our model provides the repeated, hands-on engagement that drives these outcomes.

### 2.2 Impact on Nonprofit Sector

Nonprofits building social impact often lack in-house technical capacity. By providing structured support, Hacker Dojo amplifies the effectiveness of organizations addressing critical needs in education, health, and environment.

### 2.3 Impact on Field of Education

The open-source curriculum component enables any maker space or school to replicate our model. We will actively support adoption through documentation, training, and community building—potentially reaching hundreds of organizations nationwide.

### 2.4 Impact on Local Community

Hacker Dojo serves as community anchor, creating intergenerational learning opportunities and pathways from economic marginalization to meaningful participation in the innovation economy.
