## PROJECT SUMMARY

### Overview

Hacker Dojo requests $250,000 over 36 months to expand access to technology education and community innovation in Silicon Valley through an integrated suite of maker-based learning programs serving youth, adults, and emerging nonprofit startups. The project addresses a critical gap: despite Silicon Valley's concentration of technological resources, equitable access to hands-on technology education remains out of reach for underrepresented communities in the region. Hacker Dojo's community-based model—combining workshops, makerspace training, youth STEM nights, and nonprofit startup support—directly confronts this disparity with a replicable, evidence-informed approach.

### Intellectual Merit

This project contributes to the field of EdTech innovation by documenting and evaluating a community-centered model for technology education that integrates formal learning objectives with informal, project-based pedagogy. The project will produce a replicable program framework and open-source curriculum adaptations, contributing to the broader knowledge base on effective community-based STEM education. Findings will be disseminated through practitioner networks and peer-reviewed publications.

### Broader Impacts

- **Direct service**: 1,200+ youth and adult learners annually through expanded workshop capacity and new makerspace programming
- **Geographic reach**: South Bay and East Bay communities currently underserved by technology education infrastructure
- **Workforce pipeline**: Pathway programs connecting participants to technology careers and entrepreneurial opportunities
- **Nonprofit capacity**: Technical training and maker resources for 50+ nonprofit organizations developing social-impact technology
- **Knowledge sharing**: Open curriculum, program evaluation frameworks, and implementation toolkits for replication by other community organizations

## PROJECT DESCRIPTION

### 1. Statement of Need

Silicon Valley generates a disproportionate share of the world's technology innovation and venture capital, yet access to technology education and maker resources remains heavily stratified by income, race, and geography. Communities in East San Jose, East Palo Alto, and the Tri-Cities area—despite their proximity to the world's leading technology hub—face systemic barriers to technology education:

- **Insufficient public investment**: School district technology curricula lag behind industry demand, and extracurricular STEM programs are disproportionately available in affluent neighborhoods
- **Cost barriers**: Commercial makerspaces and coding bootcamps charge membership fees that exclude low-income families ($100–$300/month typical membership cost)
- **Cultural alienation**: Tech industry recruitment pipelines rarely reach schools in underrepresented communities, reinforcing cycles of exclusion
- **Infrastructure gaps**: Community organizations wanting to offer technology programming lack access to equipment, trained instructors, and curriculum resources

Hacker Dojo was founded in 2009 to address precisely this gap. For over fifteen years, the organization has operated on a sliding-scale, community-supported model that prioritizes access over revenue. The proposed project extends this model with intentional geographic and demographic expansion, building on demonstrated demand and proven programming.

### 3. Program Description

The proposed project funds three interconnected program tracks:

#### Track A: Youth STEM Pathways Program

**Objective:** Increase access to hands-on technology education for youth ages 8–18 in East San Jose, East Palo Alto, and surrounding communities.

**Components:**
- **Monthly Youth STEM Nights**: Free evening events offering rotating hands-on projects in electronics, coding, robotics, and physical making. Each event serves 30–50 youth with parental involvement built into the format.
- **Summer Maker Intensive**: A 4-week full-day summer program offering deep-dive projects in collaboration with local industry volunteers. 40 youth per summer cohort.
- **School Partnership Program**: Weekly in-school maker workshops at 4 Title I elementary and middle schools, bringing equipment and instruction directly to students who cannot travel to the hackerspace.

**Evidence Base:**
Youth STEM Nights launched as a pilot in 2022 with grant support from the Stanford School of Engineering Community Outreach program. Initial evaluation showed a 34% increase in self-reported technology confidence among participants after 6 months of regular attendance.

#### Track B: Adult Workforce Development

**Objective:** Provide job-relevant technology skills training for unemployed and underemployed adults, with emphasis on career pathways into the technology sector.

**Components:**
- **Foundational Tech Literacy**: 8-week evening course covering computer fundamentals, productivity software, internet safety, and introduction to programming. Open enrollment, sliding-scale tuition.
- **Makerspace Certification Program**: 12-week intensive training in shop safety, equipment operation, and fabrication skills leading to independent makerspace access certification. Partnership with local employers to define competency standards.
- **Nonprofit Technology Corps**: Volunteer program pairing skilled technology professionals with nonprofits needing technical assistance (website development, data systems, operational technology).

#### Track C: Nonprofit Startup Support

**Objective:** Lower barriers for nonprofit organizations developing technology-driven social impact projects.

**Components:**
- **Makerspace Access Grants**: Subsidized full-access memberships for nonprofit organizations (501(c)(3) or equivalent) working on technology projects with community benefit.
- **Technical Consultation Hours**: Monthly office hours with volunteer engineers and designers offering project consultation.
- **Collaborative Project Space**: Dedicated hours and equipment access for multi-nonprofit collaborative technology projects addressing shared community challenges (e.g., environmental monitoring, community communication tools, accessibility technology).

### 6. Sustainability Plan

The project is designed to build organizational capacity that outlasts the grant period:

- **Earned revenue diversification**: Sliding-scale tuition for adult programs, membership fees for makerspace access (scaled to income), fee-for-service school partnership agreements
- **Corporate partnership development**: Equipment donations and volunteer engagement from technology companies; sponsored workshop programs
- **Grant diversification**: Leveraging NSF-funded program outcomes to pursue state STEM education funds, foundation support, and government workforce development contracts
- **Fee-for-service replication**: Training other community organizations to implement the Hacker Dojo model, with consulting and curriculum licensing revenue
- **In-kind support**: Ongoing relationship with Stanford School of Engineering for volunteer instructors; City of Mountain View community services partnership

### 7. Dissemination Plan

- **Open-source curriculum release**: All curriculum materials published under Creative Commons license (CC BY-SA 4.0) on the Hacker Dojo website and STEM education repositories
- **Program toolkit**: Comprehensive implementation guide for organizations replicating the model, including equipment specifications, curriculum outlines, staffing models, and evaluation frameworks
- **Conference presentations**: Submission to NSF STEM Education conference, CUE (Computer Using Educators), and National Maker Faire
- **Peer-reviewed publication**: Research article on community-based maker education outcomes submitted to *Journal of Science Education and Technology* or *International Journal of STEM Education*
